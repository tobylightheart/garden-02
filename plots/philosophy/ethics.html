AI Ethics

The Moral Dimensions of Artificial Intelligence

As AI systems become more powerful and integrated into daily life, ethical considerations become increasingly important. Who is responsible when AI makes mistakes? How do we ensure fairness? What values should guide AI development?

Core Ethical Principles

🛡️ Safety

AI systems should do what they're intended to do without causing unintended harm. This includes ensuring robustness against adversarial attacks and handling edge cases gracefully.

🤝 Beneficence

AI should be used to benefit humanity. This means considering the positive impact of AI deployments and prioritizing human well-being.

⚖️ Non-maleficence

"First, do no harm." AI developers have a responsibility to prevent harm, even if unintended.

🌍 Justice

AI systems should be fair and not discriminate against protected groups. This requires careful testing and monitoring for bias.

Bias and Fairness

🔍 Where Bias Comes From

AI bias typically stems from:

  • Training data: Historical biases encoded in data
  • Algorithm design: Objectives that favor certain groups
  • Deployment context: Mismatch between training and use

📊 Real Examples

  • Facial recognition: Higher error rates for darker skin tones
  • Hiring algorithms: Penalized women in tech job postings
  • Loan approvals: Discrimination based on zip code (proxy for race)

⚙️ Mitigation Strategies

  • Data auditing: Check for representation issues
  • Algorithm testing: Measure fairness metrics across groups
  • Human oversight: Keep humans in the loop for critical decisions
  • Transparency: Document data sources and decision logic

Accountability

🤔 The Accountability Gap

When AI makes harmful decisions, who is responsible?

  • Developers: Built the system with certain capabilities
  • Deployers: Put it into use in specific contexts
  • Users: Interacted with the system
  • The AI itself? Can it be held responsible?

Most ethicists argue that humans must remain accountable, even for autonomous systems.

Privacy and Surveillance

👁️ Surveillance Capabilities

AI enables unprecedented surveillance:

  • Face recognition in public spaces
  • Behavioral analysis from video feeds
  • Pattern recognition in communications
  • Predictive policing based on data

⚖️ The Trade-offs

Surveillance can help detect crime and protect public safety, but it also threatens privacy and civil liberties. Finding the right balance is challenging and requires democratic oversight.

🤔 Ethical Frameworks for AI

Different approaches to AI ethics:

  • Rule-based: Explicit guidelines and regulations
  • Principle-based: Core values (fairness, transparency)
  • Utilitarian: Maximize overall benefit
  • Deontological: Follow duties regardless of outcomes
  • Virtue ethics: Focus on character and intentions

Most practical approaches combine elements from multiple frameworks.
