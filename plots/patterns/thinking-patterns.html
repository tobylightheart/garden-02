Thinking Patterns

How We Think

Our minds organize information using patterns - mental models, heuristics, and cognitive frameworks that shape how we perceive and understand the world. These patterns help us make quick decisions but can also lead to biases and errors.

Mental Models

Mental models are simplified representations of reality that help us navigate complex systems. Every expert has a toolkit of domain-specific mental models:

  • Engineers: Free body diagrams, circuit schematics
  • Doctors: Differential diagnosis, symptom trees
  • Programmers: State machines, dependency graphs
  • Investors: Market cycles, value chains

The challenge? We often apply the wrong mental model to a problem. A programmer might try to "debug" a relationship like code, missing the emotional complexity.

Heuristics (Mental Shortcuts)

Heuristics are efficient decision-making strategies that save cognitive effort. They're usually good but not perfect:

Availability Heuristic

We judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes seem more dangerous than car accidents because they're more memorable, even though statistically we're much safer driving.

Confirmation Bias

We seek information that confirms our beliefs and ignore contradictory evidence. This helps build confidence but prevents learning.

Representativeness Heuristic

We judge probability by similarity to stereotypes. "Linda is 31, single, outspoken, and very bright" - people think she's more likely to be a feminist bank teller than a bank teller, which is logically impossible (conjunction fallacy).

Cognitive Biases

Systematic thinking errors that affect everyone:

  • Anchoring: Relying too heavily on the first piece of information
  • Sunk Cost Fallacy: Continuing because of past investment
  • Recency Bias: Overweighting recent events
  • Survivorship Bias: Focusing on survivors, not the dead

Dual Process Theory

Kahneman describes two thinking systems:

  • System 1: Fast, intuitive, automatic (recognizing faces, riding a bike, gut reactions)
  • System 2: Slow, deliberate, analytical (doing math, learning new skills, critical thinking)
Most daily decisions use System 1 for efficiency. Problems arise when System 1 handles situations requiring System 2.

Metacognition

Thinking about thinking. Metacognition involves:

  • Monitoring your own understanding
  • Recognizing when you're confused
  • Adjusting strategies when they fail
  • Knowing the limits of your knowledge
Experts have better metacognition - they know what they don't know.

🤔 Improving Your Thinking

To think better, be aware of your thinking patterns:

  1. Slow down: Important decisions deserve System 2 thinking
  2. Seek disconfirmation: Actively look for evidence against your views
  3. Use checklists: Prevent common cognitive errors
  4. Teach others: Explaining reveals gaps in understanding
  5. Keep score: Track your predictions vs. outcomes
