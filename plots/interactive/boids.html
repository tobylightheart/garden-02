Boids Simulation

Flocking Behavior from Simple Rules

The Boids simulation demonstrates emergence - complex global behavior arising from simple local rules. Created by Craig Reynolds in 1986, it shows how a flock of birds can emerge without any central control.

The Three Rules

Each boid follows three simple rules:

🔄 Separation

"Don't get too close to neighbors." Each boid steers to avoid crowding local flockmates. This prevents collisions and maintains the flock's loose structure.

🧭 Alignment

"Steer toward the average heading of neighbors." Each boid aligns its velocity with the local average velocity. This creates smooth, coordinated movement.

🎯 Cohesion

"Steer toward the average position of neighbors." Each boid moves toward the center of mass of nearby flockmates. This keeps the flock together.

🤔 Emergence in Action

Notice how no single boid "knows" about the flock. Each one only responds to its immediate neighbors. Yet the collective behavior looks remarkably like real bird flocking! This is emergence - complex patterns from simple rules.

This principle applies far beyond birds:

  • Ant colonies finding shortest paths
  • Stock market dynamics
  • Opinion formation in societies
  • Neural network learning
